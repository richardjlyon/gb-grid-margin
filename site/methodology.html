Methodology & sources

Every figure traces to a settled or live figure from Elexon, NESO or DUKES. No modelled numbers. This page sets out, figure by figure, the source, the formula in words, and the known limitations — including the two we disclose rather than hide: the conservative lower bound and the cross-year artifact.

On this page: Denominator · Nameplate · Verdict pair · Capacity trap · Gas vs wind · Wind stripe & counter · The history store

The denominator — national demand

Every live share divides by national demand, reconstructed from the supply side so the shares sum to 100% by construction:

national demand = positive transmission generation (excl. interconnectors and pumped storage) + net interconnector imports (all INT*, exports net off) + embedded solar (NESO) + embedded wind (NESO)

Pumped-storage pumping is demand, not supply, so it is excluded; interconnector exports net off inside net imports. A build-time guard reconciles this against Elexon's ITSDO (+ the same embedded estimate) and fails past a 12% gap — a tripwire for a gross feed failure, not a precision claim.

Nameplate (installed capacity)

Source: DUKES 2025, Table 6.2 · UK, end-2024 · full installed (not de-rated)

Wind 32.082 GW (onshore 16.166 + offshore 15.916), solar 18.28 GW — 50.4 GW of wind + solar combined. These are UK-wide; the small NI capacity makes a published share mildly conservative, never inflated. Which denominator powers which figure matters: the static capacity trap and the wind stripe divide by this DUKES figure; a live solar capacity-share would instead use NESO's GB, DC-basis figure (~22 GW, 2026) — the two bases must never be mixed.

The firm-power gauge — firm vs weather & imports

Source: Elexon FUELINST (5-min) + NESO embedded forecast · live in the browser · cadence: ~5 min

The cut. We group supply not by fuel but by what matters for reliability — whether you can call on it when you need it:

Firm power = gas + nuclear + biomass + hydro & other firm fuels Weather & imports = wind + solar + net interconnector imports

Each is a share of national demand; the two partition demand exactly, so they sum to 100%. The gauge needle reads the firm-power share and arms red when it falls below 40% — a fuel-gauge metaphor, low meaning the grid is leaning hard on sources that can vanish together. The verdict is computed identically in the build (Python) and the browser (JavaScript), pinned by a parity gate.

Why imports sit with wind and solar, not with firm power. Wind is correlated across ~1,000–2,000 km: a winter blocking high becalms Britain, France, Germany and the Benelux at once. In exactly those hours every connected market is short, prices spike everywhere, and the interconnectors run flat or reverse — imports fail precisely when they are needed. So wind, solar and imports are the correlated-failure bucket: they fall away together. Gas, nuclear and biomass do not share that weather correlation.

Why biomass sits with firm power. Biomass (chiefly Drax) is dispatchable and weather-independent — operationally it behaves like gas, available in the calm. By the only test this gauge applies — can you call on it when the wind dies? — it is firm. The separate case against biomass (its subsidy and its carbon accounting) is a question of cost, not reliability, and lives on the Subsidy Clock; we keep the two arguments in their own lanes.

Firmness, not this instant. The gauge reads a 5-minute snapshot, and on a breezy day imports and wind are real power flowing right now. "Weather & imports" is a statement about what is guaranteed when the continent is also becalmed — not a claim that those megawatts are absent at this moment. We classify by what you can count on in the tail, and we say so.

The capacity trap

Source: Elexon FUELINST + NESO output ÷ DUKES 6.2 nameplate · live ÷ quarterly nameplate

Formula in words: live wind + solar output as a share of the 50.4 GW of wind + solar Britain has installed. The numerator is live GB output; the denominator is DUKES UK installed capacity — a slightly large denominator, so the share is mildly conservative. (We deliberately do not use NESO's live embedded-wind capacity here: it is the embedded slice only, and would read a nonsensical share.)

Gas vs every wind farm

Source: Elexon FUELINST — total WIND vs CCGT + OCGT, same snapshot · live

Formula in words: the whole wind fleet's metered output against the whole gas fleet's, from one snapshot. The card is shown only while gas genuinely out-produces all wind. The per-station version ("one gas plant beats the lot") needs per-unit data (Elexon B1610) and is a deliberate fast-follow — asserted only when true.

The wind stripe & the failure counter — a conservative lower bound

Source: Elexon FUELHH (settled) ÷ DUKES 6.2 nameplate (annual-step) · daily

Formula in words: each day's wind capacity factor is the mean transmission wind output that day ÷ total installed wind nameplate for that year. The counter tallies days below 10% and 5% of capacity.

Why it is a lower bound (disclosed, not hidden): FUELHH wind is transmission-metered only — it excludes embedded (distribution-connected) wind output — while the denominator is total installed wind. So the true load factor is higher than the figure shown; we publish the conservative number and say so.

The cross-year artifact. The understatement is largest in early years, when more wind was embedded and off the transmission meter; as offshore (all transmission-metered) grew, the apparent capacity factor rises. That upward drift is largely a denominator-mix artifact, not improving wind — and the all-time records cluster in the earliest, most-understated years. Read the stripe within a year, never as a cross-year trend.

The history store

Settled half-hourly FUELHH and demand from Elexon back to the clean-data edge of 2016-01-01 (nothing earlier exists on the modern API). 77 days are genuine Elexon non-publications, recorded in a frozen manifest; we never fabricate the missing half-hours. The live clock is anchored to Elexon's server-stamped snapshot, not the device clock. Full notes and the open source are in the repository.
